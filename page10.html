📖 Reading & Use of English

8 parts · 56 questions · Tests grammar, vocabulary, and reading comprehension at C1 level.

📝 Part 1 – Multiple-choice cloze

8 questions · 1 mark each. A text with 8 gaps; choose the correct word (A, B, C, or D) for each gap. Tests vocabulary: collocations, phrasal verbs, fixed phrases, linking words.

VocabularyCollocationsKey skill: precision

🔤 Part 2 – Open cloze

8 questions · 1 mark each. A text with 8 gaps; fill each with one word only. Tests grammar: articles, prepositions, pronouns, auxiliary verbs, linking words.

GrammarFunction wordsKey skill: accuracy

🔁 Part 3 – Word formation

8 questions · 1 mark each. A text with 8 gaps; use the given base word to form a new word (prefix/suffix). Tests morphology: nouns, adjectives, adverbs, verbs, negative forms.

MorphologyPrefixes & SuffixesKey skill: word families

🔄 Part 4 – Key word transformations

6 questions · up to 2 marks each. Complete the second sentence so it has a similar meaning to the first, using the given key word (3–6 words). Tests grammar and vocabulary rephrasing.

ParaphrasingStructuresKey skill: reformulation

📚 Part 5 – Multiple-choice (long text)

6 questions · 2 marks each. Read a long text and answer multiple-choice questions. Tests global understanding, detail, inference, tone, attitude, and text organisation.

Reading comprehension2 marks each

📋 Part 6 – Cross-text multiple matching

4 questions · 2 marks each. Read 4 short related texts; match prompts to the correct text(s). Tests comparison, synthesis of opinions, and critical reading.

Critical reading2 marks each

📰 Part 7 – Gapped text

6 questions · 2 marks each. A text with 6 removed paragraphs; insert them in the correct gaps. Tests text structure, coherence, and cohesion.

CoherenceDiscourse2 marks each

🔗 Part 8 – Multiple matching

10 questions · 1 mark each. Read one or several texts; match each question to the correct section. Tests scanning for specific information and detail.

ScanningDetail
💡 Top Tip: For Part 4 (key word transformations), never change the key word. Use exactly 3–6 words including the key word. Contractions count as two words (e.g., don't = 2 words). Practise with reported speech, passive voice, conditionals, and wish/if only structures.

✍️ Writing

2 tasks · 220–260 words each · Equal marks (20 each)

📝 Part 1 – Compulsory Essay

Write an essay discussing two of three given bullet points, stating which is more important and giving reasons. 220–260 words. Must be discursive and well-structured.

Discursive essayFormal register

📬 Part 2 – Choice of Task

Choose one from three options (situationally based):

  • Letter/Email – formal or informal
  • Proposal – persuasive, formal, with headings
  • Report – factual, formal, with headings/sections
  • Review – descriptive & evaluative, semi-formal

220–260 words. The question includes context, purpose, and target reader.

💡 Writing Checklist: Plan for 5–10 minutes. Use clear paragraphs. Include an introduction and conclusion. Use a range of C1 linking devices (e.g., furthermore, nevertheless, in stark contrast, consequently). Check for errors in the final 5 minutes.

🎧 Listening

4 parts · 30 questions · ~40 minutes · Each recording played twice

1️⃣ Part 1 – Multiple choice (short extracts)

6 questions · 1 mark each. Three short unrelated extracts; two questions per extract. Tests gist, detail, attitude, opinion, and context.

2️⃣ Part 2 – Sentence completion

8 questions · 1 mark each. A monologue (lecture/talk); complete sentences with a word or short phrase. Tests listening for specific information.

3️⃣ Part 3 – Multiple choice (long)

6 questions · 1 mark each. A conversation with 2+ speakers; answer multiple-choice questions. Tests understanding of interaction, attitude, and opinion.

4️⃣ Part 4 – Multiple matching

10 questions · 1 mark each. Five short themed monologues; match each speaker to the correct option. Tests gist, main idea, and parallel meaning.

⚠️ Watch out: Distractors are common! Speakers may mention words from all options. Listen for the overall meaning, not isolated words. Spelling must be correct in Part 2 (minor misspellings accepted if recognisable).

🗣️ Speaking

4 parts · ~15 minutes · Usually with a partner (or group of three)

👋 Part 1 – Interview (2 min)

Examiner asks each candidate questions about themselves: hobbies, work/study, travel, future plans. Give extended answers (not just yes/no).

Personal topics

🖼️ Part 2 – Long turn (4 min)

Each candidate gets 3 pictures and a prompt. Speak for 1 minute comparing two of them and answering the question. The partner answers a short follow-up question (~30 sec).

ComparingSpeculating

💬 Part 3 – Collaborative task (4 min)

Work with your partner. Discuss a mind-map or prompts for 2 minutes, then try to reach a decision in 1 minute. Tests turn-taking, negotiating, and interactive communication.

InteractionNegotiation

🎤 Part 4 – Discussion (5 min)

Examiner leads a broader discussion related to the Part 3 topic. Express and justify opinions, agree/disagree, and develop ideas.

OpinionJustification
💡 Speaking Criteria: You are assessed on Grammatical Resource, Lexical Resource, Discourse Management, Pronunciation, and Interactive Communication. Use a range of C1 structures: inversions, cleft sentences, mixed conditionals, hedging language.

🔧 Essential C1 Grammar

These structures frequently appear in Use of English Parts 2 & 4, and are expected in Writing & Speaking.

🔄 Conditionals & Mixed Conditionals ▼
  • Zero: If + present, present (general truths)
  • 1st: If + present, will + infinitive (real future)
  • 2nd: If + past, would + infinitive (unreal present)
  • 3rd: If + past perfect, would have + past participle (unreal past)
  • Mixed: If + past perfect, would + infinitive (past condition → present result)
  • Inverted: Had I known, … / Were she to ask, … / Should you need, …
📌 Inversions & Cleft Sentences ▼
  • Negative adverbs: Never have I seen, Rarely does one find, Not only … but also, No sooner … than, Hardly … when
  • Cleft: It is/was … that/who … (e.g., It was John who broke the vase.)
  • What-clauses: What I need is a holiday. / What happened was that we got lost.
📝 Passive & Causative Forms ▼
  • All passive forms: is said to be, was believed to have been, will be being built
  • Causative: have/get something done (e.g., I had my car repaired.)
  • Reporting verbs: It is thought that… / She is alleged to have…
🧩 Modal Verbs (advanced) ▼
  • Deduction: must have, can't have, might have, could have
  • Obligation/necessity: needn't have vs. didn't need to
  • Alternatives: be bound to, be likely to, be supposed to, be to (formal)
🔗 Linking Words & Cohesive Devices ▼
  • Addition: furthermore, moreover, in addition, not to mention
  • Contrast: whereas, while, nevertheless, albeit, despite the fact that
  • Cause/effect: consequently, as a result, owing to, on account of
  • Emphasis: indeed, in fact, undoubtedly, needless to say

💯 Top Exam-Day Tips

Practical advice to maximise your score across all papers.

⏱️ Time Management

Reading & Use of English: Spend ~10 min on Part 1–4 (quick wins), then ~50 min on Parts 5–8. Writing: 10 min planning, 35 min writing, 5 min checking per task. Listening: Use the pause to read ahead. Speaking: Keep talking – silence loses marks.

✅ Answer Sheet Discipline

Transfer answers carefully. For multiple-choice, fill the lozenge completely. For open cloze (Part 2), write one word only in CAPITAL LETTERS. For sentence completion (Listening Part 2), check spelling.

🧠 Mental Preparation

Get a full night's sleep. Bring your ID and confirmation. Arrive early. During the exam, if you get stuck, move on and return later. Never leave a blank – guess if you must. In Speaking, be a good partner: listen and respond naturally.

⚠️ Common Pitfalls: (1) Writing over 260 words – you'll lose marks for lack of concision or run out of time. (2) Using informal language in essays/reports/proposals. (3) Not reading the instructions fully – e.g., choosing one bullet point instead of two in the essay. (4) Forgetting that Listening Part 2 answers must fit grammatically into the printed sentence.
